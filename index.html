Chair: Stephen Shennan

  • 16:00–16:25 Margharita Colucci
    Abstract

    Understanding processes of early human adaptation and spread in Africa has largely focused on climate and environmental predictors, but the presence and spread of diseases and their vectors were also fundamental source of selection in the late Pleistocene. Vector presence is shaped by both climate and human behavior (i.e., land use, avoidance strategies), creating feedbacks that affect human health and evolution. An example of such complex dynamics is Plasmodium falciparum-induced malaria, transmitted by Anopheles mosquitoes, that led to the rise of resistance mutations in the human genome (e.g., sickle-cell disease). This project investigates the human-mosquito interactions as a proxy of malaria impact on human evolution and past hunter-gatherer groups in sub-Saharan Africa around 74000 to 5000 years ago. We model the distribution of three Anopheles vector groups and reconstruct the ranges of hunter-gatherer, examining paleoclimate and environmental data, epidemiological information and signatures of human activity. We are able to map through space and time the areas where malaria had the potential of impacting humans and infer possible consequences on human spatial organisation. Our results highlight the importance of considering disease distributions when modeling past human demography, demonstrating that factors beyond climate underlay population structure, patterns of habitat choice, and dispersal.
