    Abstract

  • 09:15–09:40 Maria Lopopolo Two pathogens, one disease: Challenges in the advancing and understanding of Leprosy
    Abstract

    Leprosy has long been attributed solely to Mycobacterium leprae, introduced to the Americas after European contact. However, recent genomic work reveals that a second pathogen that causes the same disease, M. lepromatosis, was already infecting humans across the continent for millennia—providing evidence of leprosy in pre‑contact America. By integrating ancient and modern genomes from both humans and animals, we can reconstruct the evolutionary history of M. lepromatosis, showing its deep diversification within the Americas and its link to wildlife hosts. These findings challenge the traditional view of a human related single emergency of leprosy and prompt a re‑evaluation of disease emergence, persistence, and zoonotic reservoirs. I will discuss the biomedical significance of recognizing two distinct leprosy agents, the implications for diagnosis and surveillance, and how new molecular‑clock approaches—accounting for lineage structure and rate variation—could reshape our understanding of the timing and evolution of leprosy worldwide.

  • 09:40–10:05 Mahan Ghafari Time-dependent evolutionary rates and the dating of ancient viral histories
    Abstract

    Reliable estimates of pathogen evolutionary timescales are central to reconstructing the origins and spread of ancient and emerging infections, yet these estimates can vary dramatically depending on the timescale over which they are measured. In this talk, I will discuss the time-dependent rate phenomenon, whereby short-term evolutionary rate estimates are often substantially higher than those inferred over deeper timescales, leading to potentially large discrepancies in age estimates for viral ancestors. I will explore the biological and methodological factors that can generate this pattern. Using examples inspired by hepatitis B virus, as well as other RNA and DNA viruses, I will show how these processes can bias molecular clock inference and, in some cases, make older pathogen origins appear artificially recent. I will argue that understanding the mechanisms behind time-dependent rates is essential for interpreting pathogen timescales in both modern and ancient datasets, and for building more robust frameworks for inferring deep evolutionary histories from genomic data.

  • 11:00–11:20 Ian Light: Integrating cross-species analysis in our understanding of LNBA lineage Y. pestis
    Abstract

    The LNBA lineage has been shown to have persisted for roughly 2,500 years across a range of over 6,000km, but much remains hidden about its manner of spread. aDNA datasets of recovered pathogen genomes are limited in their ability to infer epidemiology, especially being heavily biased towards human-derived samples. The joint analysis of human- and animal-derived pathogen genomes opens a possibility for making more informed hypotheses regarding the dynamics of the LNBA lineage.

  • 16:00–16:25 Margherita Colucci
    Abstract

    Understanding processes of early human adaptation and spread in Africa has largely focused on climate and environmental predictors, but the presence and spread of diseases and their vectors were also fundamental source of selection in the late Pleistocene. Vector presence is shaped by both climate and human behavior (i.e., land use, avoidance strategies), creating feedbacks that affect human health and evolution. An example of such complex dynamics is Plasmodium falciparum-induced malaria, transmitted by Anopheles mosquitoes, that led to the rise of resistance mutations in the human genome (e.g., sickle-cell disease). This project investigates the human-mosquito interactions as a proxy of malaria impact on human evolution and past hunter-gatherer groups in sub-Saharan Africa around 74000 to 5000 years ago. We model the distribution of three Anopheles vector groups and reconstruct the ranges of hunter-gatherer, examining paleoclimate and environmental data, epidemiological information and signatures of human activity. We are able to map through space and time the areas where malaria had the potential of impacting humans and infer possible consequences on human spatial organisation. Our results highlight the importance of considering disease distributions when modeling past human demography, demonstrating that factors beyond climate underlay population structure, patterns of habitat choice, and dispersal.
